About me

My name is Eduardo Pérez Pellitero. Originally from Barcelona, I studied audiovisual engineering and a Master in Telecom and Management at UPC, including a stay at Visics research group in KU Leuven.

I started my PhD in October 2012 in Technicolor's Image Processing Lab in close collaboration with Leibnitz Universität Hannover, under the supervision of Prof. Dr.-Ing. Bodo Rosehnhan and Prof. Dr.-Ing. Javier Ruiz as co-supervisor.

My research interests include super-resolution, machine learning, sparse coding techniques, 3D reconstruction and super-resolved texture mapping.

Some of the things I enjoy most in my life are: skipping complex math when first-reading papers, playing the electric bass and practising classical fencing.

Fast single-image super-resolution with filter selection

J. Salvador, E. Pérez-Pellitero, A. Kochale.
ICIP 2013 

@INPROCEEDINGS{Salvador2013c,
  author = {Salvador, J. and Pérez-Pellitero, E. and Kochale, A.},
  title = {Fast single-image super-resolution with filter selection},
  booktitle = {Proc. IEEE Int. Conf. on Image Processing},
  year = {2013},
}

We extend prior work on single-image super-resolution by introducing an adaptive selection of the best fitting upscaling and analysis filters for example learning. This selection is based on local error measurements obtained by using each filter with every image patch, and contrasts with the common approach of a constant metric in both dictionary-based and internal learning super-resolution

Object detection using the chains model

E. Pérez-Pellitero
Master Thesis, KU Leuven 2012. 

@MASTERSTHESIS{PerezPellitero_MsT_2012,
  author = {E. Pérez-Pellitero and R. Benenson and L. Van Gool},
  title = {Object detection using the chains model},
  school = {KU Leuven},
  year = {2012}
}

This thesis focuses on part-based object detection using as a source of information not only the appearance of the parts themselves, but also the context supplied by other surrounding parts (either inside or outside the object to detect), in opposition to the most used approach of recognizing the object using as a source of information only the appearance of the object itself.

Data

Choosing a super-resolution testing dataset can be somehow challenging. In the literature there is not standard or de facto datasets, and sometimes is difficult to find the original version of some commonly used images. In this section I will provide the datasets I have came across and used during my research, and I hope to be able to spend more time on this in the near future.

  • Kodak dataset, 24 images of 768x512 px.
  • Berkeley Segmentation Data Set(BSDS500), 500 images of 320x480 px containing some of the most used images in literature.
  • J. Yang training set, a training dataset with a growing use in sparse super-resolution algorithms. Composed by 69 images which have been cropped selecting high frequency content.
